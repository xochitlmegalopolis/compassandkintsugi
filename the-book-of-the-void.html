I am standing in an ancient forest, millions of years old, growing out of rocks whose age has passed the billion mark. The individual trees are not that old — perhaps a few hundred years at most, but they are tall — so tall, that below them one feels as small as an insect. This world, by contrast, feels enormous and eternal. Space yawns out in all directions, space empty and space made of being, space old and wise, space heavy with time’s thick breath and with implications. Plants spring up in layers from carpets of leaves and twigs and earth; and there before me, deepening the dark of night, laps the surface of water. Across it pulsing cacophonies of frogs drone out, in a near-deafening immersion of sound; and amid the shadowy towering silhouettes of the great pines and oaks flicker glimmering yellow fireflies. They dart: above, below, and to the sides, like freed and soul-possessed reflections of the stars. The cosmic bodies move too, but slowly, and bound to their courses, while the tiny beetles dart and weave, light on the air’s coiling streams. All is magic here, in the darkness, and I watch with rapt attention, staring into the black void, and feeling the chaos of shimmering life wash over me. My sight is limited, but the shadows and the silhouettes arouse my imagination; I can hear, I can feel, and I am a part of something bigger than myself while still remaining every bit myself — an individual communing, as an equal partner in a respectful and mutually loving relationship, with every other individual being that lends its voice to the now of the moment. The renowned samurai Miyamoto Mushashi called the final chapter of his Book of Five Rings “The Book of the Void”; and it is about the contrast between the world of the seen and the world of the unseen. Miyamoto Musashi was a warrior. His life’s craft was killing other men in an intensely hierarchical and competitive feudal society, and he wrote his book about what he called the “Way of Strategy”. It is full of wisdom on the arts of self-mastery and discernment, but its basis is violence and war — the art and science of destroying others’ lives in order to secure one’s own priorities, and the priorities of one’s social betters: masters and lords. For many centuries most human material societies have considered such arts and sciences necessary evils, or perhaps not even evils at all, but goods, because they have accepted as a foundational premise that it is okay and even moral to engage in sabotage, deception, and destruction in order to achieve certain socially transcendent, or contextually beneficial, ends. They have accepted and even welcomed a social order that dictates that some human beings must be subjugated to others in deference, for a variety of possible reasons — which include and have included birthright, ethnicity, raw coercive force, intelligence, creativity, wealth, factuality, organizational capacity, moral wisdom, proximity to God, and any number of other value-implicative distinctions. Members of competing factions each give their loyalty to their own basis, and, in general, believe that basis should possess exclusive right-of-way. It is an expense logic, though: someone or something must always give way, because reality must, in the end, become ordered and linear; in order for one priority to be realized, another must always be sacrificed. Thus it becomes — whatever its initial intent — a logic of blindness. Something always must be willfully confined or erased in order to make room for what is affirmed and validated. The whole is never seen as a complete whole — or, if and when it is, the proportions are always reproduced asymmetrically. The “Way of Strategy” is the way of the warrior, and the way of the warrior is a way of implicit bias. Its goal is not to seek truth and completeness, but to vanquish or contain its opposition. It will coincide sometimes, undoubtedly, with real truth, but it is not guided ultimately by truth because it is defined and given impulse by factional urgencies. “Opposition” can be defined here as anything that seeks to occupy shared vertical “coordinates” as an existing entity, while remaining independent and self-organized. That is, the “Way of Strategy” or the Way of the Warrior is not a way of partnership or plurality; it demands right-of-way to influence. In contrast to the samurai’s “Way of Strategy” I put forth the “Art of Being”. The “Art of Being” treats life not as a linear and ordered singular path, but as a multidimensional and resonant landscape. It is not based on a logic of expense, but on a logic of abundance. It does not aim to subjugate or vanquish opposition, but to thrive from the mutual synergy that holistic simultaneity creates. I would not say of this art that it is “the True Way”. I hope I would never be as arrogant as many others have been. Instead I would say: this “Art of Being” has merit because it is both nourishing and powerful; because instead of enriching a few at the expense of many, it opens the possibility of enriching everyone. You can choose to believe what I write here. Or don’t. You can accept the premises; or don’t. I have acquired sufficient proof of them during my own life for my own purposes. They will prove themselves, to the people who see their potential; and maybe they will not, to the people who have not figured out how to thrive from embracing them. I know it is possible to do things this way, because I have proven it is possible in my own life, through trial and error and experience. But it is not automatically successful. Neither, though, it must be said, is anything else. What else can I say, then? It would be nice, I think, to leave behind the warrior and his feudal, territorial, and competitive way, even if there may be many valuable things we have learned from it — we can carry them with us. I have dedicated my life to trying to imagine a way forward into the darkness of the void of the unseen, in the hopes that something better is possible among its tangled forests than what has so far been portrayed, in human history, as “necessary”. My hope is that people might one day be able to stop saying to me, “Yes, well, the world we have is imperfect, but its evils are necessary; and, after all, you know...we have nothing else.”
